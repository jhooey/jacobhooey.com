Conceptualizing the World Trade Center

In understanding the World Trade Center, it's vital to study the process from its origin until construction, as these factors have a considerable influence upon the design of the WTC. Political wars, economical considerations, and even jealously are often factors which alter the fate of a building.

After World War II, the reconstruction of Europe, the abundance of material and overall development lead to an increase in international transatlantic trade. A New York legislature in 1946 instigated the creation of a World Trade Corporation to offer a trade center in Manhattan. New York was the home to many corporate headquarters that had ties to export and imports throughout the world, so the placement of a trade center there would be most beneficial. The initial plans of the building costed $140 million and would include 5 million square feet of space. This project was never completed as it wasn't profitable due to lack of demand.

Eventually, since the economy was growing in Lower Manhattan David Rockefeller chose it as the site in 1958 for reconstruction. He "had a vision of a bustling downtown (Gillespie, page 32)" and according to his organization's proposal in the only way to achieve that was through infrastructure reconstruction and a World Trade Center.

A basic plan of the building was soon formulated. It was to be a trade center costing $250 million sitting on 13 acres of land. The tower itself was planned to be a seventy story office building all built upon " a two-story platform that would displace the conventional street grid." (Cohen 14) (Figure 1)


Figure 1: 1960 Proposal for a World Trade Center (Robins, pg 13)

Port Authority, an organization picked by Rockefeller to govern the building of TRC, helped modify the plan into a $355 million, multiple-building project with a concourse and a plaza on 16 acresof land. This was presented to the governors of New York and New Jersey, and the Mayor of New York on March 1961. (Figure 2, Figure 3, Figure 4)


Figure 2: Model of the 1961 East Side proposal for a WTC (Robins 9)


Figure 3: Artist's Rendering of Proposed East Side Plan, 1961. (Robins 15)


Figure 4: Top left: Site Plan of East Side Proposal (Above: Cross Section Perspective of East Side Proposal and Below: Lever house)

Everyone gave their approval except the governor of New Jersey, Robert Meyner. He wanted the Port Authority, who specialized upon transportation construction, to recover the rail system apparent in New Jersey before he would give allowance to the WTC. In the end Tobin, the leader of Port Authority, despite getting the shorter end of the stick, decided to take up the project. In fact, this decision greatly affected the WTC.

Tobin's staff realized that the location of Hudson and Manhattan (two old office towers) that was part of the rail system was a very ideal location for the trade center. This new site had subway routes better than the original site, and would connect the trade center directly to New Jersey. Furthermore, this move from east to west side of lower Manhattan was in great favor to the new governor of New Jersey, Hughes. All the legislations were taken care of by March 1962.

Economically, it was very difficult to lease the office space out to corporations. Money was direly needed as building costs were extremely high. Yet despite the immense amount of space that would be available "there simply were not enough...tenants." (Gillespie, 46) In addition, the legislation rules needed "75% of tenants to either be directly involved in international business or in servicing it" (Gillespie, 46). Together these two reasons gave way to Tozzoli, the head of section of Port Authority in charge of WTC, to wanting to build the highest building in the world. This attraction-like quality, and physical existence as a monument would attribute the structure with importance, status, wealth, and allure. Tozzoli thought that to attract tenants that met criteria, "the only way... was to build the world's biggest project." (Guy F. Tozzoli, interview with the author, New York City, 15 November 1994).

In 1964, the project's plans were announced. It would be a $350 million project with ten million square feet of space, in the form of "two 110-story towers that would be the tallest buildings on earth. ("Biggest Building in World to Rise at Trade Center," New York Times, 19 January 1964).

Many factors contributed to the conceptualization and finalization of the World Trade Center. Whether they were factors loyal to the original ideas, or assimilated to the political program, more and more decisions were made to snowball the size of the project, ultimately resulting in the ambitious twin towers.
